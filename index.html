Process and Cautions

These visualizations were created with data collected from EdSight , Connecticut State Government’s Database for educational data. All schools within the Hartford region were grouped by type and matched with EdSight data to find each individual school’s per-student spending. To find per-student spending by type, all of the schools were grouped by their type. Then within each type the per-student spending total amounts from each school were summed and averaged. This provided the average per-student spending by type. The values in the visualizations are averages. There are cases where a magnet school or charter school may be spending significantly more per student than a traditional public school. While Edsight is a reliable source it is not fully comprehensive. Throughout my data collection, several schools and types of schools were missing. While Figure 1 and Figure 2 show four types: CREC magnet, neighborhood, HPS magnet and charter, these are not the only school types Hartford region students can choose from. There are three technical high schools in the Hartford region focusing on vocational and technical education. They are a part of the Connecticut Technical Education and Career System (CTECS) which is not included in the Edsight data. As of 2017, these schools have begun to transisition away from the Connecticut State Department of Education (Connecticut’s Technical Education). Instead the CTECS is is set to become its “executive branch agency” (Connecticut’s Technical Education) in 2023. This is most likely why they are not included in the EdSight database. These technical schools are also not funded in the same way that neighborhood, magnet and charter schools are. They do not receive funding through the Education Cost Sharing formula and instead are “state-operated and funded out of the resources of the State of Connecticut’s General Fund" (Connecticut’s Technical Education). CTECS per-student expenditures are also calculated in a different way than other public schools. Teacher retirement and other additional expenses are included in this student spending figure. With no effective way to extract the pure per-student amount away from the total, technical schools will not be considered for this data story.

Special needs schools were also not included in the EdSight data. It is noted in the data that “all amounts include regular and special education” (“Per Pupil Expenditures”). Some of the special needs spending may be wrapped up in the same value as the regular education spending for a particular school. All of the special needs schools are a part of the Capitol Region Education District except for the iGOAL 1 - Breakthrough Magnet School, North. It is considered a magnet school but has a specialized program for special needs students. While there was data for Breakthrough Magnet School, there was not specific data for the iGOAL 1 school. It is possible the special need spending is also included in the regular school value.

School codes were used for matching Hartford region schools to the data from EdSight. This means that even if a school’s name were written differently or had changed, the data would still be retrieved. Several schools have special programs within them, for example, “iGOAL 2 - Kennelly School.” These special programs contain a different school code than the school they are associated with. This differentiates them as a separate or different school. Data for these types of schools was not found but data for their associated school was. To continue the example, data for the iGOAL 2 - Kennelly School was not found but data for just the Kennelly School was available. A majority of the unmatched special program schools were neighborhood public schools. These unmatched schools included several Pre-K, RISE and iGOAL schools. The Hartford Public High School also did not have data for all 4 years as in SYE 2018 and SYE 2019 it was split between three different school codes and names like HPHS Engineering and Green Technology Academy, HPHS Law and Government Academy and HPHS Nursing and Health Sciences Academy. These discrepancies are why in visualization 1 it is reported that only 47% of the neighborhood schools in the region were surveyed. See "Notes for PPE Data" for a full comprehensive list of schools and types that were not included due to insufficient data. While matching data using school codes allows for name changes and differing spelling, this method is not 100% effective with catching changes in schools. In the coming weeks more research will be done on these special school cases to see if they are separately funded schools and if specific student spending data is available for them.
